After Saying Women On Medicaid Should Be Sterilized, Russell Pearce Resigns From Arizona GOP

The Huffington Post   |  By Paige Lavender
Posted: 09/15/2014 9:54 am EDT Updated: 1 hour ago
Former Arizona state Sen. Russell Pearce, the architect of Arizona's controversial immigration law S.B. 1070, testifies on Capitol Hill in Washington, Tuesday, April 24, 2012, before the Senate Immigration, Refugees and Border Security subcommittee hearing entitled "Examining the Constitutionality and Prudence of State and Local Governments Enforcing Immigration Law." The Supreme Court will referee another major clash between the Obama administration and the states Wednesday as it hears argument | ASSOCIATED PRESS

Former Arizona state Senator Russell Pearce resigned as Arizona Republican Party's first vice chair late Sunday after receiving criticism over recent comments he made about women on Medicaid.

Pearce made the controversial comments on his weekly radio show.

"You put me in charge of Medicaid, the first thing I'd do is get [female recipients] Norplant, birth-control implants, or tubal ligations," Pearce said, according to the Phoenix New Times. "Then, we'll test recipients for drugs and alcohol, and if you want to [reproduce] or use drugs or alcohol, then get a job."

Pearce said "people out there [who] need help" should get it from "family, church, and community," not the government.

According to the Washington Post, Pearce said the comments were "written by someone else" and said he "failed to attribute them to the author."

"This was a mistake," Pearce said. "This mistake has been taken by the media and the left and used to hurt our Republican candidates."

The Arizona Republic reports several Republicans in the state, including nominees for governor, secretary of state and attorney general, criticized Pearce for the remarks after Arizona Democratic Party Executive Director DJ Quinlan pushed for them to do so in a news release.

Read more on the controversy over Pearce's comments at the Arizona Republic.

UPDATE -- 1:31 p.m.: Quinlan told The Huffington Post "it's a good thing" Pearce resigned.

"Unfortunately it takes kind of an embarrassment for these Republican candidates to distance themselves from Russell Pearce," he said.

Quinlan also questioned why Republicans gave Pearce such a high position in the state GOP, noting Pearce lost a recall election in 2011 and has a "whole history" of making controversial statements.

  • The Basics: Male condoms are simple, really -- a thin layer of latex or plastic that goes on the penis during intercourse. They reduce the risk of STIs by virtue of the fact that they create a barrier. They also protect against pregnancy by keeping sperm from entering the vagina. Efficacy: The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) estimates that use of male condoms is associated with a pregnancy rate of 18 percent. The organization cautions that this figure is based on how condoms are usually used, not perfect use -- which means it takes into account people who use them incorrectly, as well as people who do use them correctly, but the method fails. A review article by James Trussell, director of the Office of Population Resarch at Princeton, Unversity put the pregnancy rate associated with perfect use -- that is, the correct and consistent use of condoms -- at 2 percent. The biggest reason for failure, explains Dr. Lauri Romanzi, clinical associate professor of gynecology at Weill Cornell Medical Center in New York City, is people just not actually using the condom. "We say, 'it doesn't work when it's in the drawer,'" she explains. Other issues can include sizing ("Use common sense," Romanzi suggests, "If a condom slips off, it's too big") as well as not removing it carefully after intercourse, which can result in leaks. Another aspect that can affect failure rates is condom quality or opting for a non-latex condom. Romanzi cautions that these can have higher rates of breakage. The Downsides: With condoms, potential allergies can be an issue (up to six out of 100 people have latex allergies, Planned Parenthood estimates, the symptoms of which can range from skin irritation to hay fever-like cramps and hives). Some people complain that condoms dull sensations, decrease pleasure and may result in sexual dysfunction. The Benefits: Condoms are cheap and readily available at most drug stores without a prescription. They can also be used with every other form of contraception (excluding female condoms) to increase their efficacy.

  • The Basics: The female condom is a plastic pouch with flexible rings on either end that is inserted deep into the vagina (or anus) before intercourse. Like male condoms, they reduce the risk of sexually transmitted diseases by creating a barrier, and help prevent pregnancy by keeping sperm from entering the vagina. "Honestly, these aren't particularly popular," admits sexual health expert Evelyn Resh, CNM, MPH. Efficacy: ACOG estimates that female condoms are associated with a pregnancy rate, based on usual use patterns, of 21 percent. The perfect use rate is 5 percent. According to Romanzi, one of the reasons why this figure is slightly higher than with male condoms is because female condoms tend to fold or get pulled out. "There's nothing keeping it gripped in the vagina," she explains, "A lot of attention needs to be paid to keeping it in place." The Downsides: Potential irritation is a biggie as far as female condoms go, as -- again -- is the potential for them to slip into the vagina during vaginal intercourse. Planned Parenthood's site points out that they can also be noisy. The Benefits: Like their male counterparts, they're relatively inexpensive (normally less than 36 5) and can be found in grocery or drug stores. Some also argue that they're good because they let women take an active role in preventing pregnancy and infection.

  • The Basics: Dental dams do not really belong on a contraception list given that they do not help prevent pregnancy, but they do warrant some mention when talking about preventing STDs. The thin strip of plastic or latex -- think a larger, rectangular condom that lies flat, or something like what your dentist might use -- can help prevent STIs during oral sex, again, by creating a barrier against skin and fluid contact. They cost around 36 3.

  • Then Basics: There are two brands currently available in the U.S. -- ParaGard (which is effective for up to 12 years) and Mirena (which is effective for up to 10). Both are T-shape devices that are inserted into a woman's uterus to prevent pregnancy. Neither protects against STDs. Efficacy: ACOG estimates that IUDs have a pregnancy rate of between 0.2 and 0.8 percent, depending on whether you opt for the copper or hormonal method. The Downsides: "There is some confusion about whether or not IUDs can be used by women who eventually want to become pregnant," Resh said. "I say absolutely. [A clinician] just takes it out and [you] can get pregnant after." "Now everyone agrees that modern IUDs are extremely safe and effective and can be used by any reproductive age women, as long as their uterus is an appropriate size," says Dr. Cullins. A few potential side effects do exist, from pain at the site where they're inserted in the uterus, to moderate spotting in the first few months. In rare cases, an IUD can slip out of the uterus, cause an infection or push through the uterine wall. The Benefits: In addition to overall efficacy and the fact that you don't have to remember to take a pill, Mirena (the hormone IUD) can reduce period cramps and decrease your menstrual flow, while ParaGard doesn't actually change your hormone levels, which may be appealing to some people. If a woman determines she wants to become pregnant, all she needs to do is schedule an appointment with her health care clinician to remove the IUD.

  • The basics: Contraceptive injection, better known as the "birth control shot," is what it sounds like -- a shot, usually administered by a practitioner once every three months, of the hormone progrestin. The FDA has approved only two injectables for use in the U.S. -- Depo-Provera and Depo-subQ Provera 104 -- and you need a prescription for both. Neither protects against STDs. Efficacy: ACOG estimates that contraceptive injection has a usual use pregnancy rate of 6 percent. Trussell estimates that the perfect use rate is 0.2 percent. The Downsides: Certain side effects can come with the birth control shot, like irregular bleeding for up to the first year of use and less common issues, like a diminished sex drive. As always, you should consult with your health care practitioner to understand all of the potential side effects before getting the birth control shot. Dr. Romanzi adds that women sometimes forget exactly when they need to get their shot, and being even just a little bit late in scheduling an appointment for the injection can result in pregnancy. The Benefits: There are many potential benefits to contraceptive injection, chief among them, the fact that women don't have to remember to take a pill. According to Planned Parenthood, there is some evidence that the shot may help prevent cancer in the uterine lining.

  • The Basics: Another of the hormonal methods, the contraceptive implant, or Implanon, also works by releasing the hormone progestin into the body. Patients have a small rod implanted into their upper arm that protects against pregnancy for up to three years. Implanon does not protect against STDs. Efficacy: ACOG estimates that implants have an associated pregnancy rate of 0.05 percent. The Downsides: Irregular bleeding can be an issue and after a year of use and some women will stop getting their period altogether (for a more comprehensive list of potential side effects, check with your provider). The implant is also relatively expensive and can cost up to 36 800. The Benefits: "Hormonal birth control methods are among the most effective methods of birth control," says Planned Parenthood's Dr. Cullins. A potential added benefit of a hormonal method like the implant is that patients don't have to worry about remembering to take a pill.

  • The Basics: Though there are differences in what hormones are contained in birth control pills and their levels, at their core, all birth control pills contain hormones that help prevent ovulation. Birth control pills must be taken at the same time every day, require a prescription and do not protect against STDs. "When most women come to me looking for birth control," says Resh, "what they really mean is that they want to be on the pill. Oral contraception is definitely the most popular of the hormonal methods." Efficacy: ACOG puts the usual use pregnancy rate associated with pill use (again, taking into account people who don't use birth control pills correctly) at around 9 percent, regardless of the formulation. With perfect use, that rate drops to around 0.3 percent. The Downsides: Again, all women should consult with their health care practitioner before deciding to take birth control pills, as potential side effects can range from irregular bleeding and breast sensitivity to much more serious (although rare) problems like blood clots and stroke. Women who are on the pill, especially those over 35, should not smoke. Also, many women find it difficult to remember to take the pill every day, something Resh says is extremely important to prevent pregnancy, particularly given that many new forms of oral contraception have much lower levels of hormones than they once did. The Benefits: Like all hormonal birth control methods, oral contraception is among the most effective means of preventing pregnancy. Dr. Romanzi adds that newer extended-cycle birth control options, which regulate womens' periods so they only occur only every 12 weeks, can be beneficial for whom they are particularly difficult or disruptive. "Some women just go through the ringer when they have their periods," she explains. "This limits that to just a few times a year."

  • The Basics: Birth control ring: Yet another hormonal birth control option, the NuvaRing is a small ring (available via prescription only) that a woman inserts into her vagina once a month to help prevent pregnancy. Birth control patch: The birth control patch, commonly known by its brand name "Ortho Evra," is a small sticker that a woman applies to her skin once a week for three weeks in a row. Neither method protects against STDs. Efficacy: According to ACOG, both the ring and patch are associated with a pregnancy rate of around 9 percent. With perfect use, Trussell estimates that those rates drop to around 0.3 percent. The Downsides: Again, many of the same side effects that are associated with other types of hormonal birth control -- irregular bleeding, breast tenderness and nausea, as well as more serious problems -- can be an issue for some women using the ring and patch. Consult your clinician before determining whether or not they're the right choice for you. Women who weigh approximately 200 pounds or more and are considering the patch are especially encouraged to consult with their doctors, as it may be less effective for them. The Benefits: As with oral contraceptives, the ring and patch can help combat acne and can result in shorter, lighter periods for many women. These methods do not require women to remember to take a pill every day.

  • The Basics: Though different, these non-hormonal birth control options -- which do not protect against STDs -- all operate by helping prevent sperm from joining an egg. The diaphragm is a shallow latex cup that is inserted into the vagina to cover the cervix. The cervical cap is a small silicone cap that is inserted into the vagina and over the cervix, preventing sperm from entering the uterus. The sponge is made from a plastic foam containing a spermicide that is inserted deep into the vagina before intercourse (it has a nylon loop attached at the bottom for removal after sex). Efficacy: ACOG estimates that the diaphragm has a pregnancy rate of around 12 percent, while the both the sponge and the cervical cap have a pregnancy rate of around 12 percent for women who haven't had a baby, and 24 percent for women who have. (For more information on that, read below.) In order to be as effective as possible, the cervical cap and diaphragm must be used in conjunction with a spermicide, which immobilizes sperm. The Downsides: Each of these methods has its own potential issues, from potential difficulties inserting the contraceptive, to vaginal dryness and irritation. Dr. Cullins cautions that women who are interested in using the cervical cap or sponge should be aware that pregnancy rates tend to be higher among those who have had children before because the cervix is larger after birth, which means the seal that these barriers create may not be as effective or strong. The Benefits: For women who are not interested in using hormonal birth control methods, these devices can be good options. They are also all small and discreet and not likely to dull sexual sensations.

  • The basics: Emergency contraception is used to prevent pregnancy after a woman has had sex either without contraception or when that contraceptive method has failed. The FDA has approved three progestin-only pills that can be used as an Emergency Contraceptive Pill (ECP) in the U.S.: Plan B, Next Choice and Ella. In 2006, the FDA approved the sale of Plan B without a prescription to people ages 17 and older. There are also combined estrogen and progestin pills, as well as emergency insertion of a copper IUD. Efficiacy: The efficacy of emergency contraception varies according to type. Princeton University's Emergency Contraception website estimates that progestin-only ECPs reduce pregnancy risks by 88 percent, and by 95 percent if they are taken within the first 24 hours after intercourse. It states that combined pills reduce the risk of 75 percent, while emergency insertion of IUD reduces risk by more than 99 percent. The Downsides: Some women may feel sick after taking an ECP. Headaches, cramps and fatigue can also occur. Dr. Romanzi adds that it is critical that people take ECPs soon after intercourse in order for them to be effective in preventing pregnancy. The Benefits: Emergency Contraception allows a woman to protect against pregnancy after the fact, which means it can be an important option if you did not use protection, if the contraception you had been using failed or in the case of rape.

  • The Basics: Spermicides come as a jelly, cream, foam or suppository. They help prevent pregnancy by stopping sperm from moving, or immobilizing them. Notably, spermicides are most effective when used in conjunction with another form of birth control, i.e., a diaphragm, cervical cap, female or male condom or even with male withdrawal. Spermicides do not protect against STDs. Efficacy: ACOG estimates that spermicides have a pregnancy rate of 28 percent. Trussell estimates that perfect use is associated with a pregnancy rate of 18 percent. The Downsides: In addition to the fact that they are not very effective when used alone, spermicides can cause irritation to the penis or vagina and some people complain that they are messy. The Benefits: Spermicides are readily available in most drugstores and, especially when used in conjunction with other forms of birth control, can help prevent pregnancy.

  • The Basics: Women whose babies breastfeed exclusively for six months after birth may use this as a form of preventing pregnancy, as the body does not release a hormone necessary for ovulation. Efficacy: Planned Parenthood estimates that among every 100 women who "practice continuous breastfeeding perfectly" exclusively for the first six months following birth -- meaning they do it at least every four to six hours depending on the time of day -- there is less than one pregnancy each year. But that number increases when women don't stick to strict guidelines about how often the breastfeed at particular times of the day. The Downsides: Breastfeeding as a form of birth control is possible for only six months after a baby is delivered, and some women may find it difficult to produce enough milk. The Benefits: It is free and does not require a woman to take hormones. It also provides multiple health benefits to the baby.

  • The Basics: "You'd be surprised at how many people rely on withdrawal," Resh says. Indeed, Planned Parenthood estimates that it is the chosen method of some 35 million couples around the world. In withdrawal, or the pull out method, a man removes his penis before ejaculation. It does not protect against STDs. Fertility Awareness Methods, which are sometimes referred to as natural family planning, can range in terms of what they actually entail, but some of the most popular include the cervical mucus method, in which women track daily changes in their cervical mucus to see when they've ovulated. There is also the basal body temperature method (women's body temperatures fluctuate during their cycle) and the calendar method, which is sometimes known as the rhythm method. Very broadly, that involves women charting their cycle on the calendar to avoid sex during their most fertile days. When all three are combined, the resulting technique is known as the "symptothermal method." Efficacy: According to ACOG, fertility awareness methods are associated with a usual use pregnancy rate of around 25 percent. Planned Parenthood estimates that withdrawal can result in up to 30 births per 100 women each year, while Trussell estimates that the pregnancy rate associated with typical use is 22 percent. The Downsides: Tracking fertility can be difficult for women who have irregular cycles and some couples may find it challenging to abstain during certain times in a woman's cycle. Withdrawal may be difficult to control and time correctly. The Benefits: Neither rely on medications or devices. Charting in particular can be appealing to some couples with particular religious affiliations and beliefs.

  • The Basics: Surgical procedures including vasectomy for men and tubal sterilization for women provide a permanent option to prevent pregnancy, although in some cases they can be reversed. In a female sterilization procedure, a provider closes or blocks a woman's fallopian tubes, while in a vasectomy, a provider closes or blocks the tubes that carry sperm. Neither method protects against STDs. Efficacy: Both procedures are among the most effective at preventing pregnancy. ACOG estimates that female sterilization has a pregnancy rate of 0.5 percent, while male sterilization has a rate of 0.15 percent. And yet aside from abstinence, no method is fail-safe. "You have to consider that there's a method failure rate no matter how consistently and appropriately you use it," Resh says, "Even when you're talking about sterilization. I once had two women come into my office with pregnancies in one week. It's extremely, extremely rare, but it does happen." The Downsides: It's not necessarily a bad thing, but certainly something that prospective patients should understand completely: vasectomies and female sterilization procedures are meant to be permanent. If you want children down the road, this is likely not the best choice for you. The Benefits: Again, of all the various contraception options, these are among the most effective, but because of their permanence, should be chosen carefully.
