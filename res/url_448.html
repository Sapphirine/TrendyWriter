Dynasty

James Wolcott
September 14, 2014

Nothing I've seen recently on TV compares to the grainy spectacle, stately unfurling, inspirational lift, and human thrall of Ken Burns's latest documentary mural, The Roosevelts: An Intimate History, premiering tonight on PBS (check your local listings, just in case your local outfit is running Miss Marple Meets Dracula or some other taste treat in the 9 o'clock slot). Running in seven longish installments, The Roosevelts bolts out of the gate begins with " Get Action," or how Teddy Got His Swagger. An asthmatic child who in young adulthood was sent reeling from a forked bolt of tragedy (his mother and wife died on the same day), Teddy Roosevelt remade himself through raw experience and gritted determination into the rootin'-tootin', forward-ho! Rough Rider and political thruster who today remains a symbol of American gusto. " Get action. Do things. Be sane," was his mantra, and " get action" is such a revealing choice of verb--not " take" action but get action, as if it were something to be acquired, grasped, a palpable force to be reined. Equally revealing is the " Be sane," for it means that a life spent passive, stewing, morose, is a recipe for madness. Remaining slumped and idle is an invitation for demons and depression to visit, so keep moving, otherwise you could end up in a locked attic. The Roosevelts doesn't ignore how TR's unslakeable appetite for adventure and new vistas boiled into war-whooping bloodlust and big-game slaughter--his white hunter exploits in Africa I find as sickening as Hemingway's now--but there's no gainsaying his vision and vitality. He may have enjoyed squeezing the trigger, but he was also the presidential father of conservation, without whom we wouldn't have all the national parks and forests that his Republican successors would so love to despoil. God, he had guts. I confess to knowing nothing about TR's post-presidential ill-fated exploration of the Amazon with son Kermit that gives episode three, " The Fire of Life," the malarial fever-dream quality of a real-life Werner Herzog tale.

Compelling as the Teddy Roosevelt saga is, it is for me the set-up, the prolonged prologue, to the true heart of this series, the improbable life and transformative reign of Franklin Delano Roosevelt. I will write about the FDR and Eleanor Roosevelt nstallments as they approach airdate--though I will advise for now that the episode four, " The Storm," devoted to his crippling attack of polio and his founding of Warm Springs, is the one you should circle most urgently on your calendar--but let me make a prejudice plain: for me, FDR is the greatest man of the twentieth century. Our twin savior, along with Lincoln. You may respectfully or disrespectfully disagree with that. That's fine. You're wrong.

And I feel even more convinced, having spent time reading Nigel Hamilton's The Mantle of Command: FDR at War, 1941-1942 and David Kaiser's No End Save Victory: How FDR Led the Nation into War, both of which I recommend as fireside reading, even if you lack a fireplace and have to make do with the theatrical illusion of a fan blowing some red strips of paper.

Main link: The Roosevelts: An Intimate History (PBS)

Joan Rivers’s Plastic Fantastic

James Wolcott
September 4, 2014

There's a lot to be said for living to an age where you don't give a faint damn anymore, and for a generation or so that's the age Joan Rivers was their entire lives. Generation X, Y, and into the Millennial beyond only know the Joan Rivers who was the red carpet scourge of E!, the host of her own show on QVC, the talk show guest and stand-up who stuck a hot fork into the pretensions and absurdities of tabloid celebrities, converting every day and every venue into her own personal Friars Roast. Those who came late to the broadcast that was her public life know little or nothing about the Joan Rivers who didn't conduct herself like the fifth and most outrageous Golden Girl, who came up in the same Greenwich Village club scene as Woody Allen and Richard Pryor, appeared in the film version of John Cheever's The Swimmer, and suffered enough personal and career blows to qualify for a Judy Garland purple heart: the falling out with Tonight show host Johnny Carson and the banishment into his permanent Siberia the subsequent failure of her own late-night show, where she was introduced during the premiere to the brassy strains of " The Bitch Is Back" the suicide of her husband, Edgar Rosenberg, months after the show's cancellation (he had been a producer on the show, the pressure of which, compounded by the heart medication he was on, may have deepened his clinical depression) and too many controversies to count that rocked her cockpit wherever she went. To anyone who came of hazy consciousness later than 1990, say, Joan Rivers has prowled the pop culture stage entirely as a self-caricature, a worthy foil for Miss Piggy of the Muppets.

The Quip and the Dead

James Wolcott
September 3, 2014

Comedy writer Ken Levine (M.A.S.H., Cheers, Frasier, and Becker among his many credits) had one of those oops moments that have haunted mankind for ages, from early Neanderthal to late Neanderthal (Rick Perry). It is one of the risks run by tossing off a casual quip only to have it turn into a clanger. In a blog post titled " The ultimate 'too soon,'" Levine writes,

An occupational hazard for comedy writers:

Last week, in my review of the new Woody Allen movie I made a Joan Rivers joke. Later that morning, when I was away from my computer and couldn’t do anything about it, word came through that she was in critical condition. I since removed the joke but there it was for about six hours. I looked like the most insensitive human being on the planet. Forget that if anyone would find it funny it would have been Joan Rivers, still it looked like a cheap shot.

Obviously that wasn’t my intent.  And as I write this, I pray for her recovery. 

But those things happen. Comedy is in the specifics and sometimes you get burned as a result.

I experienced a similar shudder recently of discovering that an offhand wisecrack I had made had been overtaken by grim events, but I was more fortunate, though it gave me a momentary fright.

In the Kindle Single I did about Louis C.K., King Louie, an essay-meditation that all the caddies are talking about as they shlep along the verdant greens. Then one night I logged onto Twitter and my timeline was a continuous stream of shocked notices and condolences responding to the news that the comic had committed suicide. I shared the shock and dismay but then, like a spear thrusting up from the memory bog, I remembered that I had a sarcastic dig at a particularly lachrymose film starring this comic and it wasn't nestled deep in the next but near the top, unmissable. It was a dig at the movie and the performance, not the performer himself, I had no way of knowing that death waited in the wings, but with the Single due to launch I realized that that didn't matter--it was a parenthetical that would poke the reader in the eye so soon after the event.

Fortunately, I was able to snip and replace the aside before the Single went live--with print I probably wouldn't have been so lucky.

Even had it slipped through, it would have been a minor, innocently-intended too-sooner, as Levine's Joan Rivers ref was. Nothing compared (as one of Levine's commenters notes) to the major tone-deaf foul-up by the PR team for TV's Sleepy Hollow, which apparently hasn't been following the horrible news out of the Mideast.

The imminence of the Emmy Awards (NBC, Monday night, August 25th), hosted by beatnik poet Seth Meyers, is the perfect, shameless opportunity for me to " plug" my spanking new Kindle Single, King Louie, a deep-sea probe of the darkening comedy and moody cross-currents of Louis C.K., whose FX series Louie is " up" for four awards, including outstanding comedy series, outstanding directing in a comedy series (director, Louis C.K.), and outstanding lead actor in a comedy series (star, Louis C.K.). As if that weren't Orson Wellesian enough, C.K. is also nominated as outstanding guest star in a comedy series for his hosting turn on Saturday Night Live. Who will he be wearing on the red carpet Monday night? I hope he doesn't try getting by with a tuxedo t-shirt, because that's the sort of thing guys think is funny but really upsets your prom date.

Now about this Kindle Single. It's resplendent with bold assertions and provocative asides that may rub some readers the wrong way, rub others the right way, and rub still others both ways, creating a galvanic skin response that leaves an extra special tingle. My defense of Jerry Seinfeld as a comic actor I expect to be controversial, though some may simply settle for calling me " goofy." My critique of C.K. and Pamela Adlon as a romantic comedy couple may met with universal assent and handfuls of confetti. So be it. Sometimes one must take a bold position even if it means nobody wanting to sit next you in the cafeteria. Fine. I'll eat my damn Jell-O with my head held high and my plastic spoon untrembling.

Anyway, I think you'll enjoy it.

These Tearful Raindrops on the Windshield That Remind Me of You

James Wolcott
August 8, 2014

Season four of The Killing (Netflix), the Scandinavian noir transplanted to Seattle for all of purgatory, didn’t have the heavy precip of previous outings—those monsoon downpours capable of washing away everything except the grimy tattoos—but its detective duo were even more waterlogged with rancor, guilt, recrimination, and still raw inner-child grievances as they attempt to solve a multiple family homicide committed in an architectural showcase of glass walls and sterile white. (The blood sprays look like unframed paintings.) The investigation never quite hits optimum momentum because Holder (Joel Kinnaman) and Linden (Mireille Enos) are being pulled back even as they’re pushing forward, their attention seesawing from past to present and back again as they uncover one mystery while covering up their own earlier crime. In the finale of season three, Linden shot—executed—her former partner and lover and the current head of Seattle’s Special Investigations Unit, James Skinner (Elias Koteas), who had turned out to be the serial killer preying on young girls who had taken to a life on the streets. The scenic lake in front of his rustic cabin was laden with their disposed corpses. Skinner’s death was suicide-by-cop—he wanted to die—but internal affairs and a potential jury might not buy that rationalization for not just hauling his ass in to face justice. Linden and Holder disposed of Skinner’s car and body in the same burial waters as his victim and vowed to stick to their story of what happened that night, but in the great tradition of Zola and James M. Cain, a killer and his accomplice with a secret eating at them eventually turn on each other with a snarl and neglect one or two details that get the bloodhounds sniffing.

The snarling made both so unsympathetic that I, for one (since I’m the only one sitting in my head), didn’t care if they got caught or not. It degenerated into Linden abusing Holder as a junkie and a loser, viciously berating whichever unfortunate family members made the mistake of breathing in her presence, while Holder effed up his own family relations with cruel taunts, began inhaling substances un-conducive fo clean living and clear functioning, and morphed into a scowling wraith in a hoodie. Not only was it tiresome having all this soiled baggage of abandonment, addiction, and personal demons dumped out in dialogue after dialogue—it’s such a neo-noir cliche at this point, this notion that everyone is damaged goods manufacturing the next generation of damaged goods—but it spoiled whatever pleasure we might have gotten in the case-solving of the family slaying because, unable to maintain a professional front, Linden and Holder became a pair of truly terrible detectives. Linden was always a borderline terrible detective, righteous and rash, her saving virtue being her tenacity—caring after everyone else had moved on. Her methods and behavior were worse here and it infected Holder, who became a terrible detective too. The two of them barged into room after room (much of the action taking place at a military academy run by Joan Allen, ramrod-postured martinet by day, swaying ballroom dancer by night) insulting potential witnesses and accusing witnesses of lying after they had barely opened their mouths, conducting interrogations in a barking, browbeating manner virtually guaranteed to be met by stonewalling on the other side of the table and get a suspect lawyered up, pronto (none of the cat-and-mouse toying and probing that’s the hallmark of great tv detectives, from Peter Falk’s Columbo to Andre Braugher’s Pembleton in Homicide to Vincent D’Onofrio’s Goren in Law and Order: Criminal Intent) and getting so bogged down in their own turmoil that the few times they perform actual teamwork—as when they search crummy gas station bathrooms for evidence—are restorative after so much splenetic psychodrama.

The true detective in this Killing is Gregg Henry’s Carl Reddick, who heretofore seemed like a somewhat sleazy, shadowy cop, willing to cut corners and partake of the donuts, the opposite of a zealous investigator with a genius complex. Here Reddick reveals himself to be a sly fox in jovial disguise who picks up on the minor discrepancies and nagging, dangling details and then pieces everything together into the picture of murder/conspiracy. Reminding me of the great Hollywood character actor Jack Carson (Mildred Pierce, especially), Henry upstages his costars in The Killing not through any camera-stealing tricks but by giving Reddick a keen-eyed cunning that gleams with satisfaction after he’s cracked the case and outsmarted his arrogant colleagues. The scene in which Reddick plunks himself down in Linden’s office and tells her point-blank that he knows she  killed Skinner—Every killer does something stupid, he says, and she stupidly returned to the scene of the crime, where she was spotted, to toss Skinner’s cell phone into the lake—is as tense and masterful as anything in Double Indemnity or any of the classic noirs, their two faces floating in black space like rival moons. It was the dramatic climax of the season unfortunately, for a sense of closure and redemption and mutual healing, none of which was wanted or needed, there was a long romantic sendoff and fadeout signaled by Linden stepping outside and being bathed in beatific sunlight, any kind of sunlight being pretty much a stranger these last four seasons. Oh well, for much of its run The Killing under executive producer Veena Sud created a moody spell, a memorable, marshy setting, and gave us an inspired partnership in Linden and Holder, so for that, much thanks.

Now when is Hulu going to put up the second season of the Danish-Swedish original of The Bridge? I can still feel the phantom pangs of Saga Noren withdrawal.

Giselle, Ma Belle

James Wolcott
August 3, 2014

No, it isn't the most burning news of the day, but the most burning news of the day is guaranteed depressing, and this isn't: On Friday,ballerina Veronika Part made a victorious debut in the title role of Giselle  at the Marinnsky Theatre in St. Petersburg, Russia. She has danced Myrta, Queen of the Wilis, for her home company, American Ballet Theatre, but not Giselle, which usually (not always) goes to younger, shorter, peachy-faced dancers who can pull off the innocent-peasant love's-first-awakening gambol at the outset. Imperially tall, in her middling thirties, Ms. Part, as I like to pretend to call her, is not one of nature's naturals for the role, but art is greater than nature, at least in the kingdom of ballet, and from the clips that have turned up on YouTube of the Friday night performance she made the role magnificently her own. Lyrical, translucent, using her long limbs to eloquent, elegant effect, emotionally open and vulnerable, VP proved the doubters wrong, as she has done for most of her career. 

Haglund at Haglund's Heel has  showcased two YouTube clips from the performance, recorded by  MagicDance46, to whom all gratitude is bestowed. 

The second clip is from Giselle's " Mad Scene," and let me note that I've seen Ms. Part play a couple of 'mad scenes' in close quarters meself, with no dance involved, and now have a special Tornado Watch app on my iPhone devoted exclusively to Veronika. The phone vibrates, her picture comes up on the screen, and I make a sign of the cross as the window panes begin to tremble...

A small price to pay for her greatness as a ballerina and artist, or at least that's what I tell myself as we huddle and wait for the all-clear siren...

(This is just my way of teasing Ms. Part, pay no serious mind.)

MagicDance46 has  other clips from act one of Giselle, which you can check out if you're so inclined, and if you're not, you probably haven't read this far anyway.

From Here to Jeter-nity

James Wolcott
July 24, 2014

The late, valiant, still-missed blogger hero Steve Gilliard, one of whose fighting credos was " Fuck the Yankees," would have relished Drew Magary's helpful and informative " The Hater's Guide to Derek Jeter" in Deadspin, which I've only just come across, having been very busy lately with my " sewing projects."  

Gilliard, I feel certain, would have relished and amen'd Magary's mocking excoriation of the Derek Jeter Retirement Victory Lap Celebration, which wallowed in a mid-season orgy of encomia at this year's All-Star Game.

Magary: 

I missed the All-Jeter Game last night but I'm told it was an emotional Jeterball game in which Derek Jeter jetered a few hits and saved 12 babies that fell from the stands and mowed the outfield so that you could see a crucifix pattern in the grass. There was also a small Jeterversy in which Adam Jeterwright laid down for Derek Jeter (who hasn't?!) and let him get off an extra Jeter for old Jeter's sake.

And with that, I think I've had enough Derek Jeter for the next 80 lifetimes... You don't need to be from Boston to feel the primal urge to run up to your rooftop and cry out FUCK DEREK JETER as loudly as possible. Not anymore. Derek Jeter can eat a bag of [salted peanuts], and here are a few reasons why.

1. Only an asshole gives himself a one-year retirement tour.  Jeter's all-star appearance last night was treated like a simultaneous memorial for eight cancer kids, even though he still has three more months of baseball to play and people give precisely zero fucks about the all-star game. OH MY GOD IT'S HIS LAST MEANINGLESS LONG WEEKEND VACATION EXHIBITION TELL YOUR GRANDKIDS. If Jeter really were Mr. Classy, he would have just up and left at the end of last season. Instead, we get a full season of breathless Jeter farewell coverage, like a retirement party for an insufferable brown-nosing co-worker, only the party lasts half a year and they've barred the exits to Ballroom C at the Best Western so you can't leave. If you wanna retire, retire.

2. Derek Jeter is a living incarnation of New York City's worst ideas about itself.  I lived in New York for six years and living in New York means you get to treat the rest of the country like it's the kiddie table at cocktail party. " Oh, Chicago? Go have a seat with Dallas and Denver over in the corner." Jeter is the perfect delivery vessel for that very specific brand of provincial arrogance. If he had played in Houston, he would have been Craig Biggio Jr. and we would have been spared all this shit. In New York, only New York matters, and so Derek Jeter gets treated as the only baseball player who matters by a cadre of slobbering New York writers who still wish athletes would get shitfaced with newspapermen at Toots Shor's...

There's this old theory that making it in New York is still a more authoritative kind of success than making it somewhere else (even though people can now work from basically anywhere). Not every athlete can cut it in NEW YORK. Oooh, the papers there are mean and stuff! It takes real backbone not to pick up a copy of the  New York Post.  And so Jeter is a living embodiment of New York's self-proclaimed higher standards. Jeter made it in New York, therefore he is better and stronger and braver than everyone else. It's grade inflation.

3. He's a useful front for all the horrible shit the Yankees do.  The Yankees can strong-arm fans who have to piss during " God Bless America," and they can bully the city into ponying up a billion for a new stadium, and they can do this in part because they can just roll out Jeter anytime they like as an ambassador of some kind of fabled Yankee Way...

Well, there's more, but I think you get the " gist."  

Yes, the Jeter Peter Meter will pop its top before we are through. But my own biggest gripe against Derek Jeter is that  super-monster-sized mansion he had built for himself in Florida  that looks like a cross between Downton Abbey, Bruce Wayne's manor, and a Russian oligarch's wet dream and nearly 31,000 square feet is big enough to house a remake of Miami Vice and three different Real Housewives series simultaneously. Nicknamed " St. Jetersburg," it's where Jeter and his humility will go to retire after he finishes this season receiving baseball nation's grateful genuflection.

Main link: Drew Magary, " The Hater's Guide to Derek Jeter," Deadspin

Beene and Gone

James Wolcott
July 16, 2014

" A few years back,"   writes Colin McDowell at The Business of Fashion, " I happened to be with Tom Ford and Mark Jacobs — separately, but within a few days of each other — and I asked them both who they felt was the greatest designer produced by the United States in the 20th century, a figure who could be placed alongside couturiers of the calibre of Chanel and Balenciaga. There was not a flicker of doubt in either man’s mind. 'Geoffrey Beene,' they both said."

Yet a decade after his death, Beene’s fashion genius and accomplishments have faded from the firmament. “How can such a thing happen with a designer, who, after just one year of designing his own label, won the first of eight Coty Fashion Critic Awards (precursor to the CFDA awards today), was the very first American designer invited to show his collection in Milan, and – less than five years after his first show in 1963 – was chosen to design the wedding dress of President Lyndon Johnson’s daughter in 1968?”

Those of us who knew Beene don’t have to fish around for an answer. “You’ve got to play the game,” as even a Zen such as John Cage conceded, and Geoffrey Beene wouldn’t play the game. He’s paying the posthumous price for his defiant opting out, though he doubtless wouldn’t want it any other way. 

I first met Geoffrey in the Eighties when, having read a review of the New York shows that I had written, he invited me to lunch. At that time, I didn’t realise that ‘lunch’ was for him a pivotal point in a day which began at 7:30 each morning when he arrived at his office with the sketches he had worked on even earlier in the morning at home, which he shared with his assistants. For a time, these assistants included, for a six-month period, Issey Miyake, and, for much longer, Alber Elbaz. When asked if Geoffrey had left him anything when he died, Elbaz replied, “He left me everything I have everything I knew, everything I learned. He taught me his world.”

Although Geoffrey was a stickler for correct behaviorr — I once infuriated him so much by being ten minutes late for lunch, he could barely speak out of fury and he made me feel like a naughty schoolboy — he was creatively totally generous and open. But his manners and attitudes were distinctly old school — surprisingly so for one who in every other way was youthful and open-minded until the day he died. But he could be prickly and difficult and you watched your step with him. He hated the vulgarity of Seventh Avenue and did all he could to undermine the arrogance of the really big designer companies. I soon realized that although the atmosphere in his company was serious and, indeed, dignified (he was always referred to as Mister Beene) there was always humour.

You felt the atmosphere the moment you stepped into the lobby. No marble, no mirrors, no impossibly chic disdainful women in black. Instead, a fun wallpaper by the artist and friend Joe Eula, whose work, along with that of another of his favorites, the illustrator Gladys Perint Palmer, amused him. And of course his receptionist Joyce, whom he adored for her quick-witted élan (people crossed oceans to pop in a say hello to her and enjoy her 500 syllables per second delivery over the phone). I loved everything about that atmosphere, kept calm with the greatest tact by the business manager Russell Nardoza, who was the perfect counterbalance to Joyce’s marvellous personality. At the centre of it all was Geoffrey Beene himself. He did not hide in boardrooms. He was a hands-on, day-to-day designer who was technically savvy and had a clear and consistent view of how the Beene customer wished to be dressed. His customers did not always find it easy to get into his world, but once there they rarely left, and they kept their Beene creations for life.

Laura and I paid our own visits to Mr. Beene’s office, where he would emerge softly shuffling from his studio as if it were a wizard’s workshop, which of course it was. On the mannequins were displayed some of his latest designs, which seemed conceived for a dynamic future better than the future we probably deserved and certainly got. He was so soft-spoken, his Southern accent so disinclined to step on any hard consonants, that I (being half-deaf) sometimes wished a Cone of Silence would drop from the ceiling to block out the ambient racket from 57th Street below, but his laughter was always bell-clear and expressed genuine amusement, never cynical irony. The brittle pettiness of so much of the fashion world was banished in his court. He enjoyed talking the latest movies, books, art shows. McDowell: 

…He had an excellent eye for fine art and sculpture. A visit to his house in Oyster Bay was a treat. He had a fine collection of 20th century art, including many works by Keith Vaughan, Pavel Tchelitchew, Duncan Grant and Man Ray. His house in Hawaii, hanging over the ocean like a liner, with every window facing the water, was equally exciting for his collection of Wiener Werkstätte art and early 20th century Austrian furniture, a testimony to him as the one of the only American designer with a knowledge and appreciation of European culture that I have ever met. [Beene was also an avid enthusiast of Asian culture and design, once expressing exasperation that the Americans in Sofia Coppola’s Lost in Translation took so little interest in getting out of their hotel comfort zones and taking in the Tokyo nights, which, he said, were more interesting than “sitting around and moping.”]

And yet, the man who enjoyed all this was happy to subvert the rules by making sequinned evening dresses based on American football jerseys and once created a collection based on cartoon characters, shown to a soundtrack of Looney Tunes music. For him, creativity was all about changing parameters, finding new ways to say old things and modernize the eternal verities of style and creativity. Looking back, he seems to me to have so much more in common with industrial designers such as Charles and Ray Eames or Eileen Gray than his fellow New York fashion designers. But perhaps he has the most in common with another great fashion designer known for his quarrels as much as his creations. I refer to Charles James, whose current show at the Metropolitan Museum makes me hope that we won’t have to wait too long for such a tribute to be made to Geoffrey Beene…

We might very well have a long wait, but let’s not go into the whys and wherefores of  that, which you can figure out yourself if you read McDowell’s tribute closely. 

However, it’s very encouraging how intelligent, appreciative, and informed the comments are to McDowell’s column at BoF, which believe me is a rarity these days, where most comments sections are overrun by orcs. 

Main link: Colin McDowell,  “Geoffrey Beene, Cultured Visionary”

Malice in Blunderland

James Wolcott
July 16, 2014

Due to a writing project that has kept me driving forward with the eyes-locked, white-line feverish resolve of Walter White racing to his latest date with destiny, I was unaware of how badly the United States has fucked the dog with one of our greatest and most valuable allies, Germany.

" Fucked the dog" is the phrase commonly used in international relations to denote a major foul-up, something I learned during my years in the diplomatic corps.

In his latest commentary, " Germany and the United States: Unprecedented Breach," the historian and geopolitical observer Immanuel Wallerstein takes ominous note of this remarkable heave-ho.

On July 10, the German government demanded the immediate departure of the head of the CIA mission in Berlin. Such demands are not unusual, even between ostensible allies. What is unusual is that it should be publicly announced, and with much fanfare. What accounts for what some are already calling an " unprecedented breach" in the very close relations after 1945 between the United States and the German Federal Republic?

What set Germany off was not a firefight involving Jack Bauer that left a half-dozen stunt men strewn across the cobblestones until the director yelled, " Cut," but further revelations of US spying of Germany which exacerbated tensions and frustrations festering from earlier bombshells about the NSA monitoring the mobile phone of German Chancellor Angela Merkel. President Obama apologized to Merkel, but the US persisted in treating a trusted ally wrong.

Wallerstein:

By now, every major or minor paper in Germany, the United States, France, Great Britain and elsewhere is featuring the story, analyzing the causes, and preaching the solution. Above all, most articles are searching for whom to blame. The principal suspects are the National Security Agency (NSA) and President Obama. But is it simply the unwise decisions of the NSA or of Obama? In other words, could it have been different? Well, surely in detail. The U.S. government has been stupid and very clumsy. However, the problem is structural and not the passing mistakes and stupidity of those in power in the United States.

The basic problem is that the United States is, and has been for some time, in geopolitical decline. It doesn't like this. It doesn't really accept this. It surely doesn't know how to handle it, that is, minimize the losses to the United States. So it keeps trying to restore what is unrestorable - U.S. " leadership" (read: hegemony) in the world-system. This makes the United States a very dangerous actor. No small number of political agents in the United States are calling for some sort of decisive " action" - whatever that could possibly mean. And U.S. elections may depend in large part on how U.S. political actors play this game.

In its technological and military arrogance and staunch sense of " exceptionalism," the US is refusing to acknowledge post-empire realities. It's as if Dick Cheney maintains a Vulcan death grip on the power establishment, and indeed maybe he does.

Main link: " Germany and the United States: Unprecedented Breach," by Immanuel Wallerstein
